# Review Rubric

Judge each paper or idea on six axes:

1. Problem significance: does the problem matter beyond a niche benchmark?
2. Novelty: what is truly new relative to the closest papers?
3. Technical depth: are the key mechanisms nontrivial and well justified?
4. Evaluation rigor: do the experiments actually support the claims?
5. Communication quality: is the paper easy to audit, reproduce, and trust?
6. Risk profile: what missing experiment, assumption, or artifact flaw could sink the paper?

Use reviewer language precisely:

- "Not novel" means a close prior work already contains the key idea.
- "Incremental" means the improvement is real but narrow or unsurprising.
- "Underspecified" means the method cannot be reproduced or falsified from the text.
- "Under-evaluated" means the paper may be good, but the evidence is insufficient.
- "Overclaimed" means the stated contribution outruns what the data proves.

When giving feedback, separate:

- Fatal issues: likely rejection causes.
- Major issues: substantial but fixable weaknesses.
- Minor issues: polish and clarity gaps.
- Questions: points that could change the recommendation if answered well.
